Study Guide 1: Safe and Effective Care Environment

This section focuses on the nurse's role in providing a safe and effective care environment for clients. It includes two main categories: Management of Care and Safety and Infection Control.

Management of Care

Management of care involves providing and directing nursing care that enhances the care delivery setting to protect clients and health care personnel.

Advance Directives

Advance directives are legal documents that allow individuals to make decisions about their future medical care in the event they are unable to do so themselves. Key types include:

  • Living Will: Specifies the types of medical treatment a person wants or does not want if they become incapacitated.
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare: Designates a person to make healthcare decisions on the individual's behalf when they are unable to.

Nurses must be knowledgeable about advance directives and respect client decisions. It's crucial to advocate for clients' rights related to these documents.

For more information, you can refer to resources on the NCSBN website.

Delegation and Supervision

Delegation is the process of transferring the authority and responsibility to another competent individual to complete a nursing task. Supervision involves monitoring the performance of the delegated task and ensuring compliance with standards of practice.

The Five Rights of Delegation are essential to remember:

  1. Right Task
  2. Right Circumstance
  3. Right Person
  4. Right Direction/Communication
  5. Right Supervision/Evaluation

Understanding these rights is crucial for safe and effective delegation to Licensed Practical Nurses (LPNs) and Unlicensed Assistive Personnel (UAP).

Ethical Practice

Ethical practice guides nursing actions and decision-making. Key ethical principles include:

  • Autonomy: Respecting the client's right to make their own decisions.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the best interest of the client.
  • Non-maleficence: Doing no harm.
  • Justice: Providing fair and equal treatment to all clients.
  • Fidelity: Maintaining promises and loyalty to the client.

Safety and Infection Control

Safety and infection control involve protecting clients and healthcare personnel from environmental hazards and preventing the spread of infection.

Infection Control

Infection control practices aim to prevent the transmission of microorganisms. This includes:

  • Hand Hygiene: The single most important practice to prevent the spread of infection. Refer to the CDC guidelines on hand hygiene.
  • Standard Precautions: Used for all clients regardless of their diagnosis. Includes hand hygiene, use of personal protective equipment (PPE) when indicated, and safe injection practices.
  • Transmission-Based Precautions: Used for clients with known or suspected infections spread through airborne, droplet, or contact transmission. Refer to CDC's information on transmission-based precautions.

Understanding the different types of precautions (e.g., airborne for tuberculosis, droplet for influenza, contact for MRSA) is vital for preventing nosocomial infections.

Accident Prevention

Preventing accidents is a key nursing responsibility. This involves:

  • Identifying clients at risk for falls, injury, or other accidents.
  • Implementing appropriate safety measures such as bed alarms, side rails (when indicated), and non-slip footwear.
  • Educating clients and families about safety precautions in the healthcare setting and at home.

Study Guide 3: Psychosocial Integrity

This section focuses on promoting and supporting the emotional, mental, and social well-being of clients and their families.

Abuse/Neglect

Nurses play a critical role in recognizing and reporting suspected abuse or neglect (child, elder, domestic violence). Key indicators and reporting procedures are essential knowledge.

  • Physical Abuse: Unexplained injuries, bruises in unusual places, burns, fractures.
  • Emotional Abuse: Withdrawal, fear, anxiety, low self-esteem.
  • Sexual Abuse: Inappropriate sexual behavior, STIs, genital trauma.
  • Neglect: Poor hygiene, malnutrition, dehydration, lack of supervision.

Nurses are often mandated reporters, meaning they are legally obligated to report suspected abuse to the appropriate authorities. Familiarize yourself with your local and state reporting requirements.

For more information and resources, you can visit the website of the Child Welfare Information Gateway or the National Center on Elder Abuse.

Mental Health Concepts

Understanding basic mental health concepts is crucial for providing holistic care. This includes knowledge of common mental health disorders (e.g., anxiety, depression, schizophrenia), therapeutic communication techniques, and crisis intervention principles.

  • Therapeutic Communication: Active listening, empathy, open-ended questions, and avoiding judgmental language.
  • Crisis Intervention: Techniques to help individuals cope with overwhelming stressors and regain equilibrium.

Resources like the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) provide valuable information on various mental health conditions.

Coping Mechanisms

Identifying and supporting clients' adaptive coping mechanisms is important for their psychosocial well-being. Nurses should also be able to recognize maladaptive coping mechanisms and help clients develop healthier strategies.

  • Adaptive Coping: Problem-solving, seeking social support, relaxation techniques.
  • Maladaptive Coping: Avoidance, denial, substance abuse. For information on substance abuse resources, visit the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA).

End-of-Life Care

Providing compassionate and respectful care to clients and their families during the end-of-life process is a critical nursing role. This includes understanding concepts like palliative care and hospice.

  • Palliative Care: Focuses on providing relief from the symptoms and stress of a serious illness.
  • Hospice Care: Provides care for individuals in the last phases of an incurable disease, focusing on comfort and quality of life.

Nurses should also be sensitive to the emotional and spiritual needs of the client and family during this time.

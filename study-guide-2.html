Study Guide 2: Health Promotion and Maintenance

This section focuses on the nurse's role in promoting and maintaining the health of individuals, families, and communities across the lifespan.

Aging Process

Understanding the normal physiological and psychological changes associated with aging is crucial for providing appropriate care. Key areas include:

  • Physiological Changes: Decreased skin elasticity, reduced muscle mass, altered sensory perception, slower reaction times, and changes in organ function.
  • Psychological Changes: Potential for social isolation, changes in cognitive function (though not all are pathological), and adaptation to life transitions.

Nurses should assess older adults for age-related changes and implement interventions to promote independence and quality of life. Resources like the National Institute on Aging offer valuable information.

Ante/Intra/Postpartum and Newborn Care

This involves providing care to women during pregnancy, labor, delivery, and the postpartum period, as well as care for the newborn. Key aspects include:

  • Prenatal Care: Education on nutrition, exercise, warning signs, and fetal development.
  • Intrapartum Care: Monitoring maternal and fetal well-being during labor and delivery.
  • Postpartum Care: Assessing maternal recovery, providing education on newborn care, and promoting bonding.
  • Newborn Care: Assessing the newborn's transition to extrauterine life, providing routine care, and educating parents.

Understanding potential complications such as preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, and postpartum hemorrhage is essential. For more information, refer to resources from the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG).

Developmental Stages and Transitions

Nurses need to be aware of the physical, cognitive, psychosocial, and moral development across the lifespan (e.g., Erikson's stages of psychosocial development, Piaget's stages of cognitive development). This knowledge helps in understanding age-appropriate behaviors and needs.

Understanding these stages helps nurses tailor their communication and care interventions to the client's developmental level.

Health Promotion Programs

Nurses are often involved in health promotion programs aimed at improving the health and well-being of individuals and communities. Examples include:

  • Smoking cessation programs
  • Weight management programs
  • Stress reduction workshops
  • Community health education on topics like nutrition and exercise.

These programs often focus on education, behavior modification, and creating supportive environments.

Immunizations

Administering and educating about immunizations is a critical aspect of health promotion and disease prevention. Nurses should be familiar with the recommended immunization schedules for all age groups. You can find the latest schedules on the CDC website.
